See <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photic_zone"" title=""Photic zone"">photic zone</a>."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spawn_(biology)"" title=""Spawn (biology)"">Escapement</a></b>"|"the percentage of a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spawning"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Spawning"">spawning</a> anadromous fish population that survives all obstacles during their migration, including fishing pressure and predation, and successfully reach their spawning grounds."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estuary"" title=""Estuary"">Estuary</a></b>"|"a semi-enclosed coastal body of water with one or more rivers or streams flowing into it, and with a free connection to the open sea. Healthy estuaries can have high rates of biological productivity."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Littoral_zone#Eulittoral_zone"" title=""Littoral zone"">Eulittoral zone</a></b>"|"another name for the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intertidal_zone"" title=""Intertidal zone"">intertidal zone</a> or <i>foreshore</i>, extending from the spring high tide line to the neap low tide line."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euryhaline"" title=""Euryhaline"">Euryhaline</a></b>"|"fish that are tolerant to a wide range of salinities."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eutrophication"" title=""Eutrophication"">Eutrophication</a></b>"|"an increase in chemical nutrients – typically compounds containing nitrogen or phosphorus – in an ecosystem. Eutrophication in water often results in an increase in algae growth and decay, which can lead to decreased levels of oxygen and fish populations."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exclusive_Economic_Zone"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Exclusive Economic Zone"">Exclusive Economic Zone</a> (EEZ)</b>"|"a seazone under the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea"" title=""United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea"">law of the sea</a> over which a state has special rights to the exploration and use of marine resources. Generally a state's EEZ extends to a distance of 200 nautical miles (370&#160;km) out from its coast.</li></ul>"
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fish_farm"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Fish farm"">Farmed fisheries</a></b>"|"are <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fishery"" title=""Fishery"">fisheries</a> where the fish are farmed using <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aquaculture"" title=""Aquaculture"">aquaculture</a> techniques. They can be contrasted with <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wild_fisheries"" title=""Wild fisheries"">wild fisheries</a>."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fecundity"" title=""Fecundity"">Fecundity</a></b>"|"the number of eggs a fish produces each reproductive cycle; the potential reproductive capacity of an organism or population. Fecundity changes with the age and size of the fish."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fish"" title=""Fish"">Fish</a></b>"|"a true fish is a vertebrate with gills that lives in water. However, in the context of fisheries, the term ""fish"" is  generally <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fishery#The_term_fish"" title=""Fishery"">used more broadly</a> to include any harvestable animal living in water, including <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mollusc"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Mollusc"">molluscs</a>, <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crustacean"" title=""Crustacean"">crustaceans</a> and <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Echinoderm"" title=""Echinoderm"">echinoderms</a>.</li></ul>"
<dl><dd><dl><dd><dl><dd><ul><li>the term ""<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shellfish"" title=""Shellfish"">shellfish</a>"" refers to molluscs</li>
<li>the term ""<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finfish"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Finfish"">finfish</a>"" refers to bony fishes, sharks and some rays</li>
<li>the term ""scalefish"" refers to fish bearing scales</li>
<li>the term ""fish"" can refer to more than one fish, particularly when the fish are from the same species</li>
<li>the term ""fishes"" refers to more than one species of fish</li></ul></dd></dl></dd></dl></dd></dl>
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fishing"" title=""Fishing"">Fishing</a></b>"|"the activity of trying to catch fish"
<li><b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fisherman"" title=""Fisherman"">Fisherman</a></b> or <i>fisher</i> - someone who captures fish and other animals from a body of water, or gathers shellfish.</li>
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fishery"" title=""Fishery"">Fishery</a></b>"|"the activities leading to and resulting in the harvesting of fish. It may involve capture of <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wild_fisheries"" title=""Wild fisheries"">wild fish</a> or raising of fish through <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aquaculture"" title=""Aquaculture"">aquaculture</a>. A fishery is characterised by the species caught, the fishing gear used, and the area of operation."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fishing_effort"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Fishing effort"">Fishing effort</a></b>"|"a measure of how much work is needed by fishermen to catch fish. Different measures are appropriate for different kinds of fisheries."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fishmeal"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Fishmeal"">Fishmeal</a></b>"|"protein-rich animal feed product based on fish."
<li>Fishing trip - usually performed by using a vessel, fishing trip starts when departing a port and ends when returning to port or in some cases when landing the catch. Fishing trip consists of different types of activities performed when fishing, for example - setting traps, recovering traps, crossing a fishing zone, relocating catch, discarding catch etc.</li>
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fishing_vessel"" title=""Fishing vessel"">Fishing vessel</a></b>"|"any vessel normally used for the harvesting of living aquatic resources or in support of such activity. This includes vessels which provide assistance to other fishing vessels such as supply, storage, refrigeration, transportation or processing (mother ships)."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fishing_fleet"" title=""Fishing fleet"">Fishing fleet</a></b>"|"an aggregation of fishing vessels of a particular country, such as the Russian fishing fleet, or using a particular gear, such as purse seine fleet."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Residence_time#Flushing_time"" title=""Residence time"">Flushing time</a></b>"|"the time required to replace all the water in an <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estuary"" title=""Estuary"">estuary</a> or <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harbour"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Harbour"">harbour</a> by the actions of currents and tides."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fish_measurement"" title=""Fish measurement"">Fork length</a></b>"|"in fishes with forked tails, this measures from the tip of the snout to the fork of the tail. It is used in fishes when is difficult to tell where the vertebral column ends."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forage_fish"" title=""Forage fish"">Forage fish</a></b>"|" are small fish which are preyed on by larger predators. Typical ocean forage fish are small, <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filter_feeder"" title=""Filter feeder"">filter-feeding</a> fish such as <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herring"" title=""Herring"">herring</a>, <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anchovies"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Anchovies"">anchovies</a> and <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menhaden"" title=""Menhaden"">menhaden</a>. They compensate for their small size by forming <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shoaling_and_schooling"" title=""Shoaling and schooling"">schools</a>. See <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bait_fish"" title=""Bait fish"">bait fish</a>."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreshore"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Foreshore"">Foreshore</a></b>"|"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intertidal_zone"" title=""Intertidal zone"">intertidal area</a> between the highest and lowest tide levels"
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Founder_effect"" title=""Founder effect"">Founder effect</a></b>"|"the loss of genetic variation when a new colony is established by some individuals moving to a new area that is unoccupied. As a result, the new population may be distinctively different from its parent population."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free-diving"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Free-diving"">Free-diving</a></b>"|"diving under water without the assistance of breathing apparatus to collect oysters, abalone, corals, sponges, crayfish etc. The gear usually includes a snorkel, face mask, flippers, weight belt and wet suit."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermocline"" title=""Thermocline"">Front</a></b>"|"region of sharp gradient in <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermocline"" title=""Thermocline"">temperature</a> or <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halocline"" title=""Halocline"">salinity</a>, indicating a transition between two current systems or water masses. Fronts are usually associated with high biological activity and high abundance of highly migratory resources such as tuna. They are actively sought as fishing areas and can be monitored by satellite remote sensing.</li></ul>"
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamete"" title=""Gamete"">Gametes</a></b>"|"eggs and sperm."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gear"" title=""Gear"">Gear</a></b>"|"the equipment used by fishermen when fishing. Some examples are hooks, lines, sinkers, floats, rods, reels, baits, lures, spears, nets, gaffs, traps, waders and tackle boxes."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene_flow"" title=""Gene flow"">Gene flow</a></b>"|"The movement of genes from one population to another by individuals moving between the populations."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghost_net"" title=""Ghost net"">Ghost nets</a></b>"|"fishing nets and other gear that has been left or lost in the ocean and continues to capture and kill fish."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gillnet"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Gillnet"">Gillnet</a></b>"|"fishing nets constructed so that fish are entangled or enmeshed, usually in the gills, by the netting. According to their design, ballasting and buoyancy, these nets can be used to fish on the surface, in midwater or on the bottom. The mesh size of the net determines the size of fish caught, since smaller fish can swim through the mesh. See also <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drift_net"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Drift net"">drift net</a>."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_positioning_system"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Global positioning system"">Global positioning system</a></b>"|"a device which uses satellite signals to accurately determine a fishing vessel's position and course."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_warming"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Global warming"">Global warming</a></b>"|"the increase in the average temperature of the Earth's near-surface air and oceans in recent decades, as well as the projected continuation of this trend."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Groundfish"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Groundfish"">Groundfish</a></b>"|"fish that lives most of its life on or near the sea bottom, such as cod, haddock, or flounder."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bay"" title=""Bay"">Gulf</a></b>"|"a large area of water bordered by land on three sides.</li></ul>"
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat"" title=""Habitat"">Habitat</a></b>"|"the place where an organism lives."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halocline"" title=""Halocline"">Halocline</a></b>"|"a zone in which salinity changes rapidly."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmful_algal_bloom"" title=""Harmful algal bloom"">Harmful algal bloom</a> (HAB)</b>"|"an algal bloom that produces toxins detrimental to plants and animals. Scientists prefer this term to <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_tide"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Red tide"">red tide</a>, since not all algal blooms are harmful, nor do all algal blooms cause discoloration, and the blooms are not associated with tides."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harvest"" title=""Harvest"">Harvest</a></b>"|"the number or weight of fish caught and retained from a given area over a given period of time. Note that landings, catch, and harvest are different."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fish_hatchery"" title=""Fish hatchery"">Hatchery</a></b>"|"the process of cultivating and breeding a large number of juveniles in an enclosed environment. The juveniles are then  released into lakes, rivers or fish farm enclosures."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_seas"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""High seas"">High seas</a></b>"|"waters outside national jurisdictions."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Highly_migratory_species"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Highly migratory species"">Highly migratory species</a></b>"|"a term which has its origins in the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea"" title=""United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea"">United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea</a>. It refers to fish species which undertake ocean migrations and also have wide geographic distributions. It usually denotes tuna and tuna-like species, shark, marlins and swordfish. See also <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transboundary_stock"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Transboundary stock"">transboundary stocks</a> and <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Straddling_stock"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Straddling stock"">straddling stocks</a>."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Husbandry"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Husbandry"">Husbandry</a></b>"|"the farming practice of breeding and raising fish stock."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypoxia_(environmental)"" title=""Hypoxia (environmental)"">Hypoxia</a></b>"|"occurs in aquatic environments when dissolved oxygen becomes depletion to a level which is harmful to aquatic organisms.</li></ul>"
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incidental_catch"" title=""Incidental catch"">Incidental catch</a></b>"|"the catch of non-fish species, caught in the course of commercial fishing practices. Examples of non-fish species are <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seabirds"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Seabirds"">seabirds</a>, and marine mammals and reptiles, such as <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dolphin"" title=""Dolphin"">dolphins</a>, <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinniped"" title=""Pinniped"">seals</a> and <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_turtle"" title=""Sea turtle"">sea turtles</a>. Incidental mortality can be contrasted with <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bycatch"" title=""Bycatch"">bycatch</a>, which is a general term for the catch of <b>all</b> fish and non-fish species other than the targeted species."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Individual_transferable_quota"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Individual transferable quota"">Individual transferable quota</a> (ITQ)</b>"|"a management tool by which the total allowable catch quota is allocated to individual fishers or companies who have long-term rights over the quota or can transfer it to others by sale, lease, or will. See also <i>quota.</i>"
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intertidal"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Intertidal"">Intertidal</a></b>"|"The intertidal zone is the region of land which is submerged during high tide and exposed during low tide."

"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invertebrate"" title=""Invertebrate"">Invertebrates</a></b>"|"animals without a backbone, such as octopus, shellfish, jellyfish and corals. See also <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertebrate"" title=""Vertebrate"">vertebrates</a>."

"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isopleth"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Isopleth"">Isopleth</a></b>"|"contour line joining points corresponding to similar values. Often used to plot yield-per-recruit values on a graph showing the changes as a function of size-at-first-capture and fishing mortality."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isopod"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Isopod"">Isopods</a></b>"|"group of small <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crustacean"" title=""Crustacean"">crustaceans</a> that includes <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fish_lice"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Fish lice"">fish lice</a>."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotherm_(contour_line)"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Isotherm (contour line)"">isotherm</a></b>"|"contour line connecting points with the same temperature.</li></ul>"

"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocean_current"" title=""Ocean current"">Ocean currents</a></b>"|"Oceanic currents can be divided into surface and deep ocean currents. Surface currents are generally wind driven and develop typical clockwise spirals in the northern hemisphere and counter-clockwise spirals in the southern hemisphere. Surface currents can operate to a depth of 400 meters and apply to about ten percent of water in the ocean. Deep ocean currents are driven by density gradients in water due to temperature (thermo) and salinity (haline) differences. This <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation"" title=""Thermohaline circulation"">thermohaline circulation</a>, occurs at both deep and shallow ocean levels and moves much slower than tidal or surface currents. Upwelling and downwelling areas in the oceans are areas where significant vertical currents of water are observed. Ocean currents can be contrasted with the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_currents"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Tidal currents"">tidal currents</a> that occur in <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coast"" title=""Coast"">coastal areas</a>."

"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standardization"" title=""Standardization"">Standardization</a></b>"|"procedures which maintain methods and equipment as constant as possible. Without standardization one cannot determine whether measurements of yearly differences in relative abundance are caused by actual fluctuations in stock abundance or by differences in the measurement procedure used. Lack of standardization is one reason why surveys using different commercial fishing vessels in different years do not produce comparable information. For example, if two vessels of different horsepower are used in separate years, the results can't be compared unless vessel mensuration experiments are performed. This would involve comparing the two vessels' catches to determine the influence of their fishing power on the size of the catch, and a determination of a correction factor."

"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocean_current#Function"" title=""Ocean current"">Surface ocean currents</a></b>"|"surface currents are generally wind driven and develop typical clockwise spirals in the northern hemisphere and counter-clockwise rotation in the southern hemisphere. In wind driven currents, the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ekman_spiral"" title=""Ekman spiral"">Ekman spiral</a> effect results in the currents flowing at an angle to the driving winds. "<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surplus_production"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Surplus production"">Surplus production</a></b>"|"Surplus production is the inherent productivity of a fish stock that can be harvested on a sustainable basis. Based on the theory that, at large stock size, reproductive rates and rate of stock growth are slowed by self-regulating mechanisms, and that stock growth rates are faster after removals, as the stock attempts to rebuild. In theory, fishing can be moderated to take advantage of the more productive stock growth rates, provided that it does not exceed the stock recovery capacity."
